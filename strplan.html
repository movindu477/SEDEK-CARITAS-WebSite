Introduction

Rooted in the Gospel, Committed to the Poor, Called to Transform

At the heart of the Church's mission is the call to love, not as sentiment, but as a powerful, transformative force that upholds the dignity of every human being, especially the poor, marginalized, and vulnerable. This love, rooted in faith and action, drives the Church's commitment to justice and compassion.As Pop Benedict XVI affirms in Deus Caritas Est, "Love-Caritas-will always prove necessary, even in the most just society"(DCE,§28). It is this enduring call that inspires and shapes the mission and work of Caritas Sri Lanka.

Purpose and Scope of the Strategic Plan (2026-2030)

Caritas Sri Lanka (CSL), the social arm of the Catholic Bishops' Conference of Sri Lanka, reaffirms its mission through this Strategic Plan (2026-2030), rooted in Catholic Social Teaching and responsive to the country's socio-economic, political, and environmental realities. Guided by Gospel values, the plan defines CSL's strategic direction as a faith-based actor committed to justice, compassion, and human dignity.

The plan outlines key thematic priorities and goals across sectors including education, livehoods, healtth, environment, disaster risk reduction, peacebuilding, and migration. It ensures alignment with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), national development strategies, and the ongoing economic restructuring agenda. Covering both dioceasan and national levels, the plan provides a unifieed framework for effective implemntation, monitoring, and learning-enabling CSL to adopt to emerging challenges while continuing to serve the most vulnereable communities with relevance and impact.

This strategic plan offers a renwed vision for CSL, inspired by the theological insights of Deus Caritas Est, Caritas in Veritate, Rerum Novarum, Laudato Si', Fratelli Tutti, and the spirit of Synodality. These documents together form the backbone of the Church's social doctrine provide a profound framework to address today's interrelated crisis: poverty, inequality, environemnt degradation, displacement, and cultural fragmentation.

1. Theological and Social Foundations: Love, Truth, and Justice

The Church's social teaching, being with Rerum Novarum(1891), establishes that the condition of the working classes and the rights of the poor are central to the Gospel mandate.Pope Leo XIII writes, "Every program of actionshould be concerned above all with the well-being of the workers" (RN,§31).CSL's work with plantation communities, war-affected families, and economically marginalized groups echoes this call, focusing on advocacy and rights promotion, especially in regions long neglected by mainstream development.

Caritas in Veritate(2009) by Pope Benedict XVI affirms that charity must be grounded in truth and justice: "Charity is at the heart of the Church's social doctrine" (CiV,§2). It is not enough to respond to symptoms of poverty; struuctural injustice must be transformed. CSL's focus on organizational capacity building and sustainable developemnt represents a deliberate effort to move from short-term relief to long-term empowerment, building systems and communities capable of advocating for their own rights and well-being.

From the theological foundation of love (agape), CSL’s work is not merely functional — it is spiritual. In the Sri Lankan context, where communities carry deep wounds from civil conflict, ethnic division, and economic hardship, love becomes a healing force. As Fratelli Tutti (2020) powerfully states: “Love shatters the chains that keep us isolated and separate; in their place, it builds bridges” (FT, §62). CSL’s peace and reconciliation programs seek precisely to build such bridges — between communities, ethnic groups, and religious traditions — offering healing and promoting a culture of encounter.

2. A Synodal Approach: Journeying Together in Mission

In the current ecclesial context, the Synod on Synodality invites the Church to walk together listening, discerning, and acting as one body. CSL’s strategic planning process embraces this spirit. The plan was shaped through dialogue with diocesan Caritas partners, local communities, Church leaders, and civil society actors, ensuring that it reflects both the voice of the poor and the guidance of the Spirit

Synodality is not a method; it is a way of being Church. As Pope Francis explains, “A synodal Church is a Church of listening” (Synod Preparatory Document, 2021). CSL remains committed to localization empowering diocesan Caritas units and community-based organizations to lead in planning, implementation, and monitoring. This decentralized approach ensures contextual relevance and ownership while remaining united in mission.

Caritas Sri Lanka As A Faith Based Organisation



Caritas Sri Lanka-SEDEC, the social arm of the Catholic Bishops' Conference of Sri Lanka, is legally recognized under Parliamentary Act No. 17 of 1983, known as the Catholic Bishops’ Conference in Sri Lanka (Incorporation) Act, affiliated with Caritas Internationalis. Caritas Sri Lanka (CSL), established in 1968, operates through 13 Diocesan Centres across Sri Lanka's 9 provinces and 25 districts, with a significant focus on areas with high Tamil population, notably in the Northern and Eastern provinces and Colombo.

Governed by a 16-member board with Bishops as Chairman and Vice-Chairman, and a 7 member advisory committee, CSL currently employs 44 staff members at the National Centre and 323 staff members across Diocesan centres. With a diverse portfolio, CSL is currently implementing 17 projects and has reached: 3,276 individuals in Environment projects, 14,017 individuals in Plantation sector projects, formed 1,142 CBOs benefiting 6,496 individuals in Food security projects, and aided 435 individuals in the Mitigation of Thalassemia disease project. Additionally, it has supported livelihood, housing, education, child nutrition, wellbeing, and peace initiatives, reaching out to thousands more.

Guiding Principles

Our work is inspired and grounded in the values of Catholic Social Teaching, which uphold the dignity of every human being and the call to act with justice, compassion, and love.

Dignity of the Human Person

We affirm the inherent dignity and equality of every human life, from conception to natural death. We unequivocally reject the marginalization of the poor, persons with disabilities, the elderly, or any vulnerable members of the human family.

Mercy and Compassion

Rooted in the Gospel, mercy and compassion guide our mission. We are called to respond to suffering with love and action, walking alongside those in pain to restore hope and dignity.

Formation of the Heart

Charity is not only a professional duty but a vocation of love. Those who serve in our mission are called to a “formation of the heart,” dedicating themselves to others with deep empathy and a genuine commitment to human dignity. This formation complements and enriches professional training.

Equal Opportunities and Responsibilities

We promote the equal dignity, rights, and responsibilities of women and men, advocating for inclusive participation and shared leadership in every aspect of society.

Preferential Option for the Poor

We prioritize the needs of those who are poor, marginalized, or vulnerable. In solidarity with them, we work to challenge the root causes of poverty and injustice, helping to build a more equitable and compassionate world.

Universal Destination of the Goods of the Earth

We believe that the earth and its resources are meant for the benefit of all. We strive to transform unjust systems and structures so that everyone can share in the fruits of creation and enjoy their basic human rights.

Solidarity

We embrace the interconnectedness of the human family. By standing with the poor and advocating for the common good, we commit to building a more just and united world.

Subsidiarity and Cooperation

We uphold the principle of subsidiarity, respecting the autonomy of local communities while fostering cooperation across our global network. A united approach enables us to be more effective in advancing our shared mission.

Stewardship of Creation

The earth is a sacred trust, given for the benefit of all. As stewards of creation, we are committed to promoting sustainable practices and inspiring care for our common home, ensuring it remains a life-giving gift for generations to come.

Tsunami Response

Over a four and a half year period from 2005, Caritas Sri Lanka provided 7,594 new houses and repaired 2,167 damaged houses, benefiting 9,761 families. Additionally, 24,000 beneficiaries received assistance through the Livelihood Assets Restoration Program, with micro-credit grants and loans totaling Rs. 167,106,205. Collaborating with various stakeholders including government bodies, NGOs, and health and counseling institutions, Caritas Sri Lanka conducted 814,592 house visits and provided psychosocial support.

Ethnic War- Rehabilitation

Caritas Sri Lanka Diocesan centres in conflict-affected areas across Anuradhapura, Batticaloa, Mannar, Trincomalee, and Jaffna provinces have undertaken extensive humanitarian efforts, including constructing 2431 permanent houses and 2440 transitional shelters. Additionally, they've provided over 10,644 livelihood packages, donated relief items to 81,564 families, and distributed non-food items to over 6000 individuals.

Economic Crisis Mitigation Measures

The "Enhancing Resilience" program by CSL in Sri Lanka has provided vital support to those affected by the prolonged economic crisis. It has reached 40,808 families with food ration packs, assisted 27,514 children with educational facilities, counseled 552 individuals, provided medical treatment to 4,709 chronically ill patients, offered psychosocial support to 1,250 individuals, and aided 9,750 people in organic food production across the island.

Religious Amity

CSL has played a pivotal role in fostering a common vision of tolerance and harmony among all religions in Sri Lanka through initiatives aimed at changing public attitudes towards religious harmony for peacebuilding.CSL's exemplary approach to Religious Amity has garnered recognition, serving as a model for organizations and countries facing similar contexts. Sharing experiences with diverse groups like UNFPA, Agiamondo, and the University of Peradeniya, Caritas has fostered unity and dialogue.

Safe Migration

Caritas Sri Lanka supports migrants at every stage—aspiring, in-service, and returnees—through legal aid, case management, and compensation facilitation. It addresses issues like contract violations, trafficking, and unpaid wages. Caritas also provides psychosocial support to migrant families and conducts awareness sessions on safe, legal migration. By collaborating with the Catholic Migration Commission, government bodies, and stakeholders, Caritas ensures rights protection and promotes informed migration practices for vulnerable communities across Sri Lanka.

Environment protection

CSL’s proactive engagement with stakeholders and communities through initiatives like the 'Stepping Stone to Safeguarding Sri Lanka's Natural Environment Project' demonstrates a commitment to environmental awareness and action, as seen in their extensive clean-up, solidarity, and tree planting campaigns. Through collaboration and media outreach, Caritas continues to champion environmental conservation and climate justice, setting a noteworthy precedent for sustainable development.

Promoting Justice and Peace

As the National Secretariat of the Justice, Peace, and Human Development Commission of the Catholic Bishops’ Conference, Caritas Sri Lanka plays a vital advocacy and educational role. It conducts awareness programs, study sessions, and campaigns on critical national issues such as elections, the 13th Amendment, the Anti-Terrorism Act, human rights, and social justice. Through these efforts, Caritas fosters democratic values, informed civic participation, and dialogue, promoting peace, justice, and human dignity across communities in Sri Lanka.

A Learning and Proactive Organisation

Caritas Sri Lanka demonstrated its proactive and learning-oriented approach through its comprehensive response to the Easter Sunday attacks ( 21st April, 2019) offering psychosocial care, medical aid, financial and educational support, and interfaith healing. Simultaneously, it engaged in evidence-based advocacy, publishing research on plantation community welfare, migration challenges, and interreligious peacebuilding. By documenting lived realities and informing policy through data-driven insights, including the Sri Lanka country profile, Caritas advances sustainable development, social justice, and resilience-building across diverse and vulnerable communities.

STRENGTHS

    1. Catholic Identity, Credibility & Legal Standing

    • Recognized as the official social arm of the Catholic Church in Sri Lanka.

    • Legally registered under Parliamentary Act No. 17 of 1983 (not NGO Act) — ensuring tax exemption and recognition.

    • Strong Catholic identity rooted in Gospel values: love, justice, peace, compassion, and forgiveness.

    • Member Organization (MO) of Caritas Internationalis with over 57 years of national presence.

    • High credibility and trust among beneficiaries, stakeholders, and all religious/ethnic communities.

    • Strong inter-religious relationships and social acceptance across faiths.

    2. Reputation, Recognition & Accountability

    • Globally recognized through the Caritas Internationalis network.

    • Achieved CIMS certification ensuring compliance with international humanitarian standards

    • Strong relationships with government, CSOs, religious leaders, and community groups.

    • Positive community feedback and appreciation for services rendered.

    3. Organizational Structure & Governance

    • National-level strategic planning frameworks aligned with CBCSL values.

    • Structured hierarchy: CBCSL → Bishop Chairman → Vice Chairman → National Secretariat → 13 Diocesan Centers (DCs).

    • Operational independence at DC level under unified national policy.

    • Availability of constitution, HR manuals, safeguarding policies and standard operating procedures.

    • Functional Board of Directors with good governance practices.

    4. Human Resources & Capacity Strength

    • Qualified, experienced, and committed staff with grassroots understanding

    • Strong organizational hierarchy with active Diocesan Directors and trained community facilitators.

    • Presence of well-established CBOs, SHGs, federations, and interfaith forums.

    • Internal mentoring and exposure opportunities, including local and overseas training programs.

    • Deep community engagement with parish and village-level volunteer networks.

    5. Infrastructure & Resources

    • Operational buildings, vehicles, IT equipment, and basic infrastructure across most DCs.

    • Network of DCs across all districts ensuring national reach and access.

    • Some dioceses have functional income-generating projects and financial reserves.

    • Ability to respond quickly to community needs and emergencies.

    6. Financial Systems & Funding Support

    • Support from Caritas donor agencies and internally managed special funds (e.g. Lent Fund, Centre Fund).

    • Existing financial management systems, with some dioceses showing sustainability potential.

    • Presence of financial infrastructure enabling project implementation and audits.

    7. Programmatic Strength

    • Holistic and interconnected project interventions: peacebuilding, livelihoods, youth, gender, climate action, health, etc.

    • Consistent implementation of development activities such as trainings, workshops, exposure visits, and community mobilization.

    • Inclusive engagement of marginalized groups through grassroots structures.

    8. Collaboration, Communication & Advocacy

    • Partnerships with Caritas agencies, local/international NGOs, INGOs, CSOs, and state institutions.

    • Active inter-religious and inter-ethnic coordination for peacebuilding and reconciliation.

    • Existing website, social media presence, and moderate advocacy engagement.

WEAKNESSES

    1. Identity, Visibility & Public Engagement

    • Misconceptions about conversion due to Catholic identity.

    • Limited media presence, marketing, and public relations strategy.

    • Weak outreach to potential funding partners or wider public.

    • Resistance to change in traditional operational models.

    2. Financial & Sustainability Challenges

    • High dependency on external donor funding with inadequate local resource mobilization.

    • Few sustainable income-generating models at the diocesan level.

    • Absence of a dedicated fundraising strategy and specialized fundraising teams.

    • Insufficient funds for marginalized communities and emergency preparedness.

    • Declining donor support in certain thematic areas.

    3. Human Resource Gaps

    • High turnover of trained staff due to job insecurity and low salaries.

    • Inability to retain or attract well-qualified, passionate individuals for social service.

    • No dedicated focus on recruiting and nurturing young or emerging leadership.

    • Shortage of specialized teams for project design, MEAL, digital management, and innovation.

    4. Technology & Digital Tools Deficit

    • Limited integration and underutilization of modern management tools (e.g., MEAL, CIMS, data systems).

    • Low digital literacy among some staff members.

    • Cybersecurity systems are weak or underdeveloped.

    • Insufficient use of digital platforms for outreach, communication, and monitoring.

    5. Operational & Structural Weaknesses

    • Uneven infrastructure and resource distribution across dioceses.

    • Absence of standardized program quality database.

    • Short-term or frequent transfers of Diocesan Directors disrupting continuity.

    • Inadequate documentation of staff transitions or institutional knowledge at DC level.

    • Limited collaboration or resource-sharing among diocesan centers.

    6. Policy & Capacity Building Gaps

    • Outdated or missing internal policies (e.g. gender policy, safeguarding, advocacy).

    • Lack of a formal organizational database for programs, beneficiaries, and outputs.

    • Irregular staff training and lack of standardized capacity-building tools and methodologies.

    • Weak strategy for staff development and long-term professional growth.

THREATS / RISKS

    1. Political, Regulatory & Legal Risks

    • Changes in government policy on NGOs and international funding.

    • Increased regulatory barriers, taxation risks, or restrictions on operations.

    • Political instability or local interference impacting project execution.

    • Geopolitical shifts affecting donor priorities or bilateral relations.

    2. Economic & Financial Instability

    • National economic crisis, inflation, and currency fluctuation reducing purchasing power.

    • Donor fatigue due to global crises, leading to reduced funding availability.

    • High competition for limited donor resources among NGOs.

    • Limited donor interest in key priority areas.

    3. Social & Cultural Risks

    • Misunderstandings about conversion motives and religious affiliation.

    • Religious extremism or intolerance in sensitive operational areas.

    • Community mistrust toward faith-based interventions.

    • Dependency mentality among communities undermining sustainability.

    4. Operational & Environmental Threats

    • Sudden project closures, reduced funding, or relocation harming community trust.

    • Migration or displacement of beneficiaries disrupting long-term impact.

    • Inconsistent support or coordination from government agencies.

    • Lack of cybersecurity and disaster risk management.

    5. Environmental & Contextual Risks

    • Natural and manmade disasters (floods, droughts, pandemics).

    • Environmental degradation, illegal resource exploitation (e.g., sand mining).

    • Post-war trauma and ethnic tensions affecting project outcomes.

    Risk Mitigation Measures for CSL

    • Engage government stakeholders early to ensure compliance and protect faith-based organizational operations.

    • Diversify donor base and build emergency reserves to reduce funding disruption risks.

    • Train staff in culturally sensitive outreach to prevent misinterpretation of religious motives.

    • Develop contingency plans to address political, financial, and security-related operational disruptions.

    • Promote community ownership through participation and planned phase-outs to reduce dependency risks.

    • Strengthen cybersecurity and disaster readiness to protect operations and sensitive data.

    • Collaborate with churches, CSOs, and authorities to enhance credibility and project sustainability.

    • Integrate climate resilience and trauma healing into projects to address environmental and postwar risks.

Sri Lanka: Country Context and Regional Disparities

Sri Lanka, a strategically located island nation in South Asia, lies just south of India in the Indian Ocean. Commonly referred to as the "Pearl of the Indian Ocean," it is celebrated for its rich cultural heritage, ecological diversity, and resilient population. With a population of approximately 22 million, the country is home to three major ethnic groups Sinhalese, Tamils, and Moors and functions as a Democratic Socialist Republic. Colombo serves as the commercial capital, while Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte is the administrative capital. The country has made significant development strides, especially following the end of a 30-year civil conflict in 2009. However, despite its progress, Sri Lanka faces acute socio-economic, political, and environmental challenges.

Economically, the country relies heavily on services (nearly 60% of GDP), along with key sectors such as apparel, tourism, and agriculture. In 2022, Sri Lanka faced a historic economic collapse driven by foreign exchange shortages, soaring inflation, and debt default. Though a partial recovery began in 2023–2024, regional inequalities remain a significant barrier to inclusive growth.

National-level indicators such as a 2023 Human Development Index (HDI) of 0.776 mask deep regional disparities. The Western Province, which includes Colombo, dominates economic output, contributing around 43.7% of GDP, with high HDI levels exceeding 0.80. It benefits from advanced infrastructure, ICT access, and employment opportunities, but also faces challenges such as urban poverty, congestion, and environmental stress.

In contrast, the Plantation Region—home to a large Indian Tamil estate worker population—suffers chronic underdevelopment. Districts such as Nuwara Eliya and Badulla report HDI levels below 0.70, with widespread poverty, educational dropouts, and limited access to services. The Northern and Eastern Provinces, which were heavily affected by the civil war, continue to experience post-conflict challenges, including land disputes, unemployment, and weak institutional support.

Addressing these disparities is vital for Sri Lanka’s long-term stability and development. A national strategy that integrates regional needs especially in conflict-affected, rural, and estate sectors—is essential to ensure equitable and sustainable progress for all Sri Lankans.

Sri Lanka HDI Analysis – 2024

In 2024, Sri Lanka retained its "high human development" status with an HDI score of 0.776, ranking 89th out of 193 countries, the highest in South Asia. Key strengths include life expectancy (75.9 years), high literacy (92%), mean years of schooling (10.8), and universal healthcare and education access, showcasing institutional resilience amid economic crises.

However, the inequality-adjusted HDI drops by 18%, revealing sharp disparities. The richest 1% control 20.5% of income, while the bottom 40% hold just 19%. Female labor force participation remains low (33%), despite strong educational parity. The Gender Development Index reflects a gap—male HDI: 0.795; female HDI: 0.755.

GNI per capita (PPP) declined to below $11,500, while poverty surged to 24% post-2022 crisis. Real wages fell by up to 22%, and food insecurity affects 5 million people. An aging population (8.1% over 65) and climate vulnerability (floods displacing 6,000 in 2024) add pressure to public systems. Digital inequality persists in rural, estate, and post-war regions, limiting access to digital services and opportunities.

Addressing economic inequality, gender gaps, aging, climate resilience, and digital inclusion is critical to sustain and advance human development in Sri Lanka



Impact of the Civil War and Post-War Context in Northern and Eastern Sri Lanka

Social and Psychological Impact

The decades-long civil war (1983–2009) caused widespread displacement, loss of life, and the breakdown of family and community structures across the Northern and Eastern provinces, particularly in Mannar, Kilinochchi, Mullaitivu, Vanni, and Batticaloa. More than 89,000 women were widowed, and countless children were orphaned or left with a single parent. Many women-headed households face severe socio-economic vulnerabilities and trauma. Post-conflict trauma remains largely unaddressed, with no systemic counselling support for war-affected individuals. The psychological effects—especially among youth born during the war—manifest in rising rates of suicide, substance abuse, family violence, and anti-social behaviour, weakening overall community cohesion.

Economic Challenges

Post-war economic recovery has been slow and uneven. Livelihoods centered on agriculture and fisheries remain underdeveloped due to damaged infrastructure, climate impacts, and lack of investment. Farmers face difficulties accessing irrigation and quality inputs, while fishing communities struggle with illegal fishing practices, destroyed mangroves, and unregulated aquaculture projects such as prawn and sea cucumber farms. Income insecurity is widespread, especially among women-headed households who often pawn assets to meet basic needs. Youth unemployment is high, pushing many into unsafe migration, drug use, or informal sectors. Micro-enterprises lack credit access and market linkages, while opportunities for value addition and collective production remain underutilized.

Governance and Institutional Gaps

Weak institutional capacity continues to hinder post-war recovery. Civil society organizations (CBOs) are under-resourced and lack the ability to influence policy or facilitate community development. Local governance structures often fail to deliver basic services such as healthcare, housing, water, and transport—especially in newly resettled and remote areas. Land disputes, unresolved cases of missing persons, and the continued detention of political prisoners have eroded trust in political processes. Disillusionment with traditional political parties has led to growing support for reform-oriented movements that emphasize accountability, justice, and inclusive development.

Cultural and Inter-Communal Tensions

Despite official narratives of peace, ethnic and religious divisions continue to undermine reconciliation. The post-war period saw renewed tensions between Tamil, Muslim, Sinhalese, and Christian communities, particularly after incidents like the 2019 Easter Sunday attacks in Batticaloa. Religious sites have become flashpoints for conflict, often exacerbated by political manipulation and lack of interfaith engagement. Cultural erosion, stigmatization of widows and ex-combatants, and lack of culturally grounded reconciliation processes further deepen divides and threaten long-term social harmony.

Environmental and Disaster Vulnerability

Environmental degradation poses a growing threat to sustainable development in the region. Climate change has led to erratic rainfall, frequent droughts, and flash floods that disrupt agriculture, displace communities, and destroy infrastructure. In Mannar Island, large-scale wind power and mineral sand mining projects are threatening biodiversity, ecosystems, and traditional livelihoods. The Vankalai Sanctuary, a critical site for migratory birds, is under threat due to poorly managed development, with limited environmental safeguards in place.

Education and Youth Development

Education remains a key challenge in post-war recovery. War disruptions, poverty, and trauma have led to high dropout rates, especially among youth. The current economic crisis has further forced many children out of school, particularly in rural areas. Schools suffer from poor infrastructure, understaffing, and lack of psychosocial support. Many youth lack skills and access to higher education or vocational training, limiting their employment opportunities and fueling frustration, migration, and social unrest. Investment in education is critical to break the cycle of poverty and rebuild war-affected communities.

Contemporary Forms of Slavery Affecting Malaiyaha Tamils in Sri Lanka

(Summary based on the report by Mr. Tomoya Obokata, Special Rapporteur on contemporary forms of slavery, UN Human Rights Council, A/HRC/51/26/Add.1)

In his official visit to Sri Lanka from 26 November to 3 December 2021, Mr. Tomoya Obokata, the United Nations Special Rapporteur on contemporary forms of slavery, revealed persistent structural and systemic inequalities affecting the Malaiyaha Tamil community. Brought from India over two centuries ago to work on British-run plantations, this community continues to face widespread discrimination, exploitative labour practices, and social exclusion. The Rapporteur’s findings highlight the urgent need for structural reforms and inclusive development.

Labour Exploitation and Inhumane Living Conditions

Malaiyaha Tamils remain highly vulnerable to contemporary forms of slavery, especially in smaller private plantations where bonded labour persists. Workers are often unable to leave due to debts owed to estate owners. Although wages were officially increased to LKR 1,000 per day in 2021, this gain was undermined by management practices such as increasing daily output targets and applying arbitrary wage deductions. These practices contribute to conditions that amount to forced labour and, in some cases, serfdom.

Housing conditions further entrench this exploitation. The community continues to live in colonial-era line houses, often without land ownership rights. These structures—typically 10 x 12 feet—house 5 to 10 individuals and lack basic infrastructure like windows, kitchens, running water, and electricity. Families often share communal latrines. Despite numerous requests to estate management, improvements remain minimal, and civil society organizations attempting to assist the community have reportedly faced harassment and surveillance.

Limited Access to Services and Systemic Discrimination



Healthcare access within plantations is critically inadequate. Many dispensaries are staffed by unqualified personnel, and no nearby hospitals exist, making emergency care both inaccessible and unaffordable. Government social welfare programs are reportedly implemented in name but seldom reach plantation communities.

Although some housing projects—including those supported by the Indian government—have been initiated, affordability is a major concern. Workers may be forced to take out large loans to access better housing, perpetuating cycles of debt and economic vulnerability.

A major barrier to justice and public service access is language discrimination. While both Tamil and Sinhala are official languages, many public officials use only Sinhala, making it difficult for Tamil-speaking workers to report abuses or access legal remedies. This linguistic divide also contributes to broader inequality in access to education, employment, and state services.

Youth, Education, and the Future of the Community

Many young Malaiyaha Tamils reject plantation work due to its exploitative nature, yet decent employment alternatives are scarce. Discrimination and limited access to vocational education mean most end up in informal sectors such as domestic work, cleaning, retail, or even sex work—roles often marked by precariousness and exploitation.

Education, though legally integrated into the national system since 1975, remains substandard. The literacy rate in plantation sectors is just 66%, compared to 92% in urban areas. COVID-19 worsened this gap. The shift to online learning left many children behind due to lack of internet access and inability to afford smartphones or computers.

The report reveals a deeply entrenched system of ethnically rooted inequality and labour exploitation affecting the Malaiyaha Tamil community. Addressing these injustices requires not only immediate policy reforms but also a commitment to inclusive, rights-based development that centers the voices and needs of marginalized groups.

Social Context in Sri Lanka: National Overview with Regional Disparities

Sri Lanka has made notable progress in human development, particularly in education and healthcare. With a national literacy rate of 95.7% and life expectancy averaging 76 years, the country performs well on many social indicators. These achievements are largely attributed to its universal healthcare system and free public education. However, beneath this national progress lie deep regional inequalities that affect vulnerable groups disproportionately.

Urban centers like Colombo in the Western Province enjoy high levels of social development, with widespread access to healthcare, education, and digital services. Yet, even in urban areas, growing inequality is evident through rising slum populations and social exclusion. In Kurunegala and Anuradhapura, youth unemployment, caste-based discrimination, and out-migration persist. Anuradhapura also grapples with high incidences of chronic kidney disease (CKDu) and malnutrition, particularly in rural areas.

The Plantation Region, especially Nuwara Eliya, Badulla, and Ratnapura, faces severe socio-economic deficits. Estate workers, primarily Indian Tamil communities, live in overcrowded line houses with poor sanitation and limited transport access. Educational dropout rates are high after primary school due to poverty and cultural constraints. Health concerns such as malnutrition, maternal mortality, tuberculosis, and HIV remain prevalent.

In the Northern and Eastern Provinces—including Jaffna, Mannar, Kilinochchi, Batticaloa, and Trincomalee—the legacy of civil war continues to impact social cohesion. Vanni suffers from widespread trauma, school dropouts, and drug abuse. Mannar and Batticaloa face early marriage, inter-ethnic tensions, and child labor. Access to healthcare is inadequate in Kilinochchi and Mullaitivu, while Trincomalee struggles with ethnic segregation in schools.

Gender disparities also compound social challenges. 25% of households are female-headed, many due to war widowhood, and districts like Ampara report female labor force participation as low as 17.1%. These households often lack access to land, income, and social protection.

Sri Lanka’s impressive national averages conceal significant regional divides. Addressing these disparities—particularly in estate, rural, and post-conflict regions—is essential for ensuring equitable and sustainable social development across the country.

Economic Context: National Overview with Regional Disparities

Sri Lanka, a strategically located island in the Indian Ocean, is undergoing a fragile yet steady economic recovery following its historic collapse in 2022. The crisis driven by foreign exchange shortages, unsustainable debt, and poor fiscal governance exposed deep structural weaknesses in the economy. In response, the government secured a USD 2.9 billion IMF bailout in 2023, followed by a critical USD 12.55 billion debt restructuring in late 2024, which restored some investor confidence, stabilized the currency, and brought inflation under control. By 2025, GDP growth has resumed cautiously, with forecasts of 5% growth, although economic vulnerabilities persist.

The economic structure is moderately diversified. The services sector contributes around 60% of GDP, led by tourism, ICT, logistics, and finance. The industrial sector, contributing 26%, is anchored in garments and apparel exports, while agriculture, though reduced to 7.5–8% of GDP, remains essential for food security and rural employment. Remittances and tourism remain critical foreign exchange sources, while export-import imbalances and high external debt—USD 57.2 billion or ~57.8% of GDP—pose ongoing challenges.

According to the labor force survey 2022 data, 49.8% of the working-age population (8.5 million) is economically active, with approximately 65.3% male and 34.7% female, or around 5.6 million and 3 million, respectively. The highest number of economically active labour is in the Rural sector, which is nearly 76%. The corresponding figures for the Urban and Estate sectors are 16% and 5% respectively.

Regional economic disparities sharply shape development outcomes. The Western Province, particularly Colombo, contributes over 41–46% of national GDP, benefiting from infrastructure such as the Colombo Port, Port City, and vibrant startup ecosystems. In contrast, Kurunegala and Anuradhapura depend on subsistence agriculture and informal labor, with incomes vulnerable to climate shocks and limited industrial diversification. Chilaw’s economy revolves around fishing and coconut farming, constrained by poor market access and limited technological integration.

The Plantation Region remains economically marginalized. Despite being central to tea production, workers face low wages, high youth unemployment, and limited access to vocational or digital skills. Microfinance debt burdens many, especially women, worsening economic precarity.

In the Northern and Eastern Provinces economic revival post-conflict has been uneven. Agriculture and fisheries dominate, but are undercut by illegal fishing, environmental degradation, and weak private investment. While Jaffna shows potential in ICT and small-scale apparel, centralized economic planning and military control over assets hinder growth. Youth unemployment surpasses 30% in several districts.

These imbalances directly impact HDI indicators by suppressing income levels, limiting access to services, and constraining equitable economic participation. Addressing them requires decentralization, inclusive planning, and targeted regional investment to ensure sustainable, broad-based growth.

Climate Vulnerability and Agriculture: National and Regional Context

Sri Lanka, an island nation in the Indian Ocean, is highly vulnerable to climate change due to its geographic location and socio-economic dependence on climate-sensitive sectors. Approximately 50% of its 22 million population resides in low-lying coastal areas, making them highly exposed to sea-level rise, storm surges, and salinity intrusion. The country frequently experiences extreme weather events, including floods, droughts, and landslides, with increasing intensity and unpredictability. These shifts pose serious risks to infrastructure, biodiversity, and human health, with direct repercussions on national development and HDI outcomes.

Agriculture, while contributing only 7.5–8% of GDP, remains a critical source of livelihood for a large portion of the rural population. However, changing rainfall patterns have disrupted traditional monsoon cycles, particularly in the dry and intermediate zones, affecting regions like Anuradhapura and Kurunegala, where farmers now struggle with irrigation stress and crop failures. Prolonged droughts in the North Central Province have increased the incidence of chronic kidney disease linked to contaminated water sources. In Chilaw, saltwater intrusion and erratic coastal weather further reduce arable land and threaten fishing-based livelihoods.

In the Plantation Region, notably Nuwara Eliya, Badulla, and Ratnapura—the impacts of climate change are felt through rising temperatures, landslides, and soil erosion, threatening tea production and local settlements. Poor land management, chemical runoff, and limited adoption of climate-smart practices exacerbate environmental degradation. While some estates have piloted organic farming and agroforestry, these remain under-resourced and poorly scaled.

In the Northern and Eastern Provinces, already struggling from the legacy of conflict, environmental degradation is acute. Mannar and Mullaitivu face severe soil erosion and mine-contaminated lands that remain unsuitable for cultivation. In Kilinochchi and Batticaloa, water scarcity and deforestation undermine both agriculture and community resilience. Trincomalee suffers from illegal fishing, coastal degradation, and a loss of mangroves that previously buffered climate shocks.

Across all regions, post-harvest losses, pest outbreaks, and market inaccessibility remain significant barriers. Without urgent investment in climate-resilient agriculture, early warning systems, and community-based adaptation, these environmental pressures will continue to erode food security, income stability, and public health, thus undermining HDI progress in already marginalized areas

Technological Context: National and Regional Disparities in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka’s technological landscape is evolving rapidly, particularly in the services sector, where ICT and telecommunications contribute significantly to the nearly 60% share of GDP. The country has gained international recognition for its growing IT and BPM (Business Process Management) exports, supported by investments in digital platforms across governance, education, and healthcare. Urban centers, especially Colombo, benefit from 4G/5G connectivity, smart infrastructure projects under the Megapolis plan, and thriving fintech, e-governance, and AI ecosystems. These advancements have improved administrative efficiency, expanded digital employment, and enhanced citizen access to services.

However, Sri Lanka faces a widening digital divide, especially in rural, estate, and conflict-affected regions. In districts like Kurunegala and Anuradhapura, digital infrastructure remains underdeveloped, with inconsistent broadband access in schools and homes. Chilaw, with its coastal isolation, suffers from severe digital exclusion, impeding access to e-learning and online services. The Plantation Region shows the deepest digital marginalization, where low-income estate workers lack affordable internet, digital skills, and devices, creating barriers to education and livelihoods.

In the Northern and Eastern Provinces, Jaffna has made moderate progress through ICT centers and university-led initiatives. Yet, districts like Mannar, Mullaitivu, and Batticaloa continue to lag due to weak connectivity, language constraints, and low uptake of digital training. Though pilot programs in smart agriculture and community ICT centres exist, they remain limited in reach and impact.

Technological underdevelopment constrains human capital formation, particularly in education and employment. The lack of inclusive access to digital tools and training widens regional and income-based inequalities, directly affecting HDI components such as knowledge and income. Bridging this gap is essential for equitable development and long-term resilience in a tech-driven global economy.

Political Context: National Overview with Regional Disparities in Sri Lanka



Sri Lanka’s political environment remains complex and fluid, shaped by decades of civil conflict and recent economic crises. The 2022 economic collapse triggered widespread protests, undermining public trust and culminating in a major leadership change highlighting urgent efforts to consolidate power and drive reforms amid ongoing governance challenges.

Governance in Sri Lanka remains highly centralized, with Colombo as the dominant hub for policymaking and resource distribution. This centralization facilitates national coordination but deepens regional inequalities. The Western Region, especially Colombo, benefits from stronger infrastructure, better services, and political influence. In contrast, rural and peri-urban areas such as Kurunegala, Anuradhapura, and Chilaw face political marginalization and elite capture, limiting equitable development despite Chilaw’s location in a relatively prosperous province.

Post-conflict Northern and Eastern Provinces continue to experience weak local autonomy under centralized control. Districts like Kilinochchi, Mullaitivu, and Batticaloa confront slow recovery, ongoing militarization, unresolved land disputes, and minimal resettlement. Demographic shifts in Mannar and Trincomalee generate social tensions, while Jaffna, the Tamil cultural heartland, remains politically distanced from Colombo’s power structures. Political marginalization is also acute in the Plantation Region, where the Indian Tamil community in districts such as Nuwara Eliya and Badulla suffers from disconnected representation, inconsistent policies, and weak labor unions.

These patterns of centralized governance, elite dominance, and regional political exclusion directly hinder equitable resource access and service delivery, suppressing human development outcomes across Sri Lanka’s diverse regions.

Legal Context: National Overview with Regional Disparities in Sri Lanka



Sri Lanka’s legal framework supports key development goals, including labor rights, environmental protection, and gender equality, with laws such as the 2016 minimum wage and climate adaptation policies. However, uneven implementation across rural, estate, and marginalized communities limits their impact. The coexistence of civil, personal, and customary laws—known as legal pluralism—creates enforcement challenges, especially in gender justice. Although legal protections against domestic violence and equal marriage age exist, variations in application among ethnic and religious groups restrict women’s access to justice.

Judicial independence and efficiency remain critical issues nationwide. Court backlogs, low legal literacy, and perceived political interference undermine timely justice. Environmental and land-use laws, though comprehensive on paper, suffer from weak enforcement, particularly in informal settlements and ecologically sensitive areas.

Regionally, Colombo in the Western Province benefits from stronger institutions and more effective enforcement of urban, environmental, and labour laws, including the Urban Development Authority Act and Coast Conservation Act. In contrast, districts like Kurunegala, Anuradhapura, and Chilaw experience poor enforcement of land tenure, labour protections, and environmental regulations. In Chilaw, fisherfolk and estate workers often lack formal property rights, increasing vulnerability and exclusion from legal protections.

The Plantation Region, faces inconsistent enforcement of the Plantation Labour Act. Estate workers remain largely landless, trapped in cycles of economic dependency without legal ownership or asset accumulation. Environmental laws under the National Environmental Act are also poorly applied, enabling land degradation.

In the Northern and Eastern Provinces, land disputes critically hinder development. Tamil returnees in Kilinochchi and Mannar face contested claims from state agencies and settlers, while limited legal aid in Mullaitivu aggravates exclusion. Military occupation and religious land disputes, especially in Trincomalee, further weaken trust in the legal system and obstruct effective resettlement and environmental protections.

Legal insecurity, weak land rights, and enforcement gaps significantly undermine social equity, sustainable livelihoods, and human development outcomes across Sri Lanka.

Gender Overview of Sri Lanka: National Context and Regional Disparities



Sri Lanka has made notable strides in gender equality, particularly in education. Female literacy rates are impressively high, ranging from 90% to 96%, positioning the country among South Asia’s leaders in female educational attainment, according to Caritas Sri Lanka. This achievement reflects sustained government investments and societal valuing of girls’ education. However, despite these educational gains, gender disparities persist in economic participation and social empowerment, underscoring complex socio-cultural barriers that continue to limit women’s full inclusion in public and economic life.

Labor force participation reveals one of the starkest gender gaps in Sri Lanka. While male participation exceeds 75%, female participation hovers around 35%, as reported by the Institute of Applied Statistics Sri Lanka (IASSL). This disparity is shaped by entrenched social norms, caregiving burdens, and constrained access to formal employment. Regionally, these differences are more pronounced. Districts such as Ampara report female labor force participation as low as 17.1%, reflecting traditional and rural settings where conservative gender roles prevail. Conversely, Nuwara Eliya shows a relatively higher rate of 45.8%, highlighting the influence of estate communities where women often engage in agricultural labor, albeit under informal conditions.

Vulnerabilities linked to gender are also intensified by socio-political histories and household dynamics. Approximately 25% of Sri Lankan households are female-headed, many a consequence of war-related widowhood, especially in conflict-affected districts like Batticaloa in the Eastern Province. These female-headed households frequently face economic insecurity, lack adequate social protection, and experience marginalization in local governance and decision-making processes. Such challenges are exacerbated in rural and estate sectors, where institutional support remains weak and poverty levels are high, limiting women’s access to livelihoods and social services.

Health and safety indicators present a mixed picture. Women generally enjoy higher life expectancy—76.9 years for females versus 71.3 years for males (IASSL)—yet face elevated risks of gender-based violence (GBV), including domestic abuse and sexual harassment. Older women in estate communities face compounded vulnerabilities due to economic marginalization and limited healthcare access. Additionally, the coexistence of multiple legal systems—civil, customary, and religious—creates complexities that often undermine women’s rights and protection, particularly in rural and minority communities, limiting their recourse against discrimination and abuse.

In summary, while Sri Lanka exhibits strong educational gender achievements, economic, social, and legal disparities persist, with significant regional variations. Addressing these challenges requires targeted policy interventions focusing on enhancing women’s economic opportunities, strengthening social safety nets for vulnerable groups, combating gender-based violence, and harmonizing legal frameworks to ensure equitable protection and empowerment of all women across the nation.

Mitigation Measures by Thematic Area

1. Human Development and Social Inclusion

Establish early intervention centers to reduce addiction, trauma, and domestic violence risks

1.1 Quality Education

Engage communities in schools to prevent dropouts and increase educational accountability.

1.2 Livelihood & Economic Empowerment

Train youth and women to prevent unemployment, unsafe migration, and loan dependency.

1.3 Health & Nutrition

Deliver nutrition and mental health support to reduce war-related and developmental health risks.

2. Environmental Protection & Climate Change Adaptation

Adopt climate-smart agriculture to mitigate food insecurity and environmental degradation.

3. Disaster Risk Reduction & Anticipatory Action

Create local disaster preparedness systems to reduce vulnerability and response time.

4. Peacebuilding, Justice & Social Cohesion

Facilitate interfaith dialogue to mitigate extremism, mistrust, and identity-based conflict.

5. Safe Migration & Reintegration

Promote civic engagement to reduce unsafe migration and increase local opportunity.
